Claude for Regulatory & Compliance Docs

How to use Claude to draft financial services documentation faster — without sacrificing the precision that regulators and auditors expect.

Where Claude adds the most value

Compliance documentation is slow not because the thinking is hard, but because the writing is. You know what needs to be said — getting it into the right structure, tone, and level of specificity takes time. Claude excels at exactly this translation problem.

Business Requirements Documents

Turning feature ideas and regulatory mandates into structured BRDs with clear functional requirements, assumptions, and out-of-scope items.

Risk Assessments

Drafting risk narratives, inherent vs. residual risk ratings, and control descriptions for new payment features.

Regulatory Change Impact Analysis

Translating a regulatory update (e.g. NACHA rule change) into a structured impact assessment for your platform.

Audit Evidence Narratives

Writing clear explanations of how a control works, who owns it, and where the evidence lives — in plain English for auditors.

Writing a BRD

Give Claude the business context, the regulatory driver, and the constraints. Let it structure the document — then you review and tighten.

BRD prompt
Write a Business Requirements Document for the following feature on our payment scheduling platform. Use this structure: Executive Summary, Business Objective, Regulatory Driver (if applicable), Functional Requirements (numbered), Non-Functional Requirements, Assumptions, Out of Scope, Open Questions. Feature: [describe the feature] Regulatory driver: [e.g. NACHA Same Day ACH rule change effective March 2025 / Reg E error resolution requirement / internal audit finding] Constraints: [e.g. must not change the Kafka message schema / must be completed before Q3 audit window / no changes to the core scheduling engine] Stakeholders: [e.g. Payments Ops, Compliance, Engineering, Banking Partners] Tone: formal, precise. This will be reviewed by compliance and engineering leads. Avoid vague language — every requirement should be testable.

Writing a risk assessment

Risk assessments require you to think systematically about what could go wrong. Claude is useful for making sure you haven't missed a risk category — and for writing the narrative clearly.

Risk assessment prompt
Write a risk assessment for the following change to our payment scheduling platform. For each risk identified, include: Risk Description, Likelihood (High / Medium / Low), Impact (High / Medium / Low), Inherent Risk Rating, Mitigating Controls, Residual Risk Rating. Change: [describe what is being changed] Platform context: We process $110B+ annually in scheduled payments across ACH and wire rails. A failure can affect entire payment batches. Risk categories to assess (cover all of these): - Operational risk (processing failures, batch errors) - Compliance / regulatory risk (NACHA rules, Reg E) - Financial risk (customer financial harm, exposure) - Reputational risk (banking partner trust, customer experience) - Technology / data risk (data integrity, Kafka message failures, Snowflake data accuracy) After the table, write a one-paragraph risk summary for the executive sign-off section.

Analysing a regulatory change

When NACHA, the Fed, or your regulator releases a rule update, the first question is always: does this affect us and how? Claude can help you structure that analysis quickly.

Regulatory change impact prompt
Analyse the impact of the following regulatory change on our payment scheduling platform. Regulatory change: [paste the key text or summarise the rule change] Effective date: [date] Our platform context: - We schedule ACH and wire payments for banking customers - We process batches through [describe your submission process] - Our cut-off times are [describe] - We use Kafka for payment message routing and Snowflake for reporting Please provide: 1. A plain-English summary of what the rule requires 2. Specific changes required to our platform (if any) 3. Changes required to our operational processes 4. Changes required to customer-facing communications 5. Timeline recommendation — what needs to be done before the effective date 6. Open questions we need to answer with our compliance team

Writing audit evidence narratives

Auditors ask: "How does this control work?" PMs often know the answer but struggle to write it in a way that is clear, testable, and free of engineering jargon. Claude is good at this translation.

Audit narrative prompt
Write an audit evidence narrative for the following control. The audience is an internal or external auditor who understands financial services but is not an engineer. Control: [describe the control, e.g. "All payment submissions are logged with a unique idempotency key to prevent duplicate processing"] How it works technically: [describe in plain terms how the system implements this] Where the evidence lives: [e.g. Snowflake table name / log system / dashboard] Who owns the control: [role, not name] How often it is tested: [frequency] The narrative should be 2-3 paragraphs: what the control does, how it is implemented, and how it is evidenced.

Three rules for compliance writing with Claude

Always review before submitting. Claude produces well-structured drafts, not final documents. Every compliance doc needs a human review — especially requirement numbering, regulatory citations, and risk ratings, which Claude may approximate.

Give Claude the regulation text, not your summary of it. If you're writing to a specific NACHA rule or Reg E provision, paste the actual rule text into the prompt. Claude will reason more precisely against the source than against your paraphrase.

Ask Claude to flag its assumptions. Add "At the end, list any assumptions you made that a compliance reviewer should verify" to any compliance prompt. This surfaces gaps you need to close before the document is final.
