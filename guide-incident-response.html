Claude for Incident Response

When a payment batch fails, every minute matters. Here's how to use Claude to move faster — from triage to stakeholder comms to post-mortem.

Where Claude fits in an incident

In a live incident, engineers are focused on the fix. As a PM, your job is communication, coordination, and documentation — and that's exactly where Claude accelerates you. Claude won't fix your Kafka consumer lag, but it will draft a stakeholder update in 30 seconds while you're on the bridge call.

Phase 1

Triage

Scope the blast radius. How many payments? Which customers? What's the financial exposure?

Phase 2

Communicate

Draft real-time updates for executives, banking partners, and ops teams at the right detail level for each audience.

Phase 3

Post-mortem

Structure the timeline, identify root cause, write the corrective action plan with owners and due dates.

Phase 1: Triage prompt

Use this immediately when an incident is declared. Fill in the brackets and paste it to Claude.

Triage prompt
We have a live incident on our payment scheduling platform. Help me scope the blast radius and structure my response. What I know so far: - Incident declared: [time] - What's failing: [describe the failure — e.g. ACH batch submissions are timing out] - First detected by: [monitoring alert / customer complaint / ops team] - Payments affected: [estimated number or "unknown"] - Rails affected: [ACH / wire / RTP / all] - Cut-off time for today's ACH window: [time] Help me: 1. Identify the key questions I need answered in the next 15 minutes 2. Estimate the customer and financial impact based on what I've told you 3. Identify who needs to be notified and in what order 4. Flag any regulatory notification obligations I should check (Reg E, banking partner SLAs)

Phase 2: Stakeholder communications

Different audiences need different messages. Claude is fast at adapting tone and detail level — give it the facts once and ask for multiple versions.

Executive update (for your CTO / Head of Payments)
Write a concise executive update for my CTO. Tone: calm, factual, in control. Length: 5 sentences max. Facts: - [What happened] - [When it started and when it was resolved / current status] - [Number of payments / customers affected] - [Financial exposure estimate] - [Root cause — if known] - [What we're doing right now]
Banking partner update (external-facing)
Write an external incident update for our banking partners. Tone: professional, transparent, no internal jargon. Do not speculate on root cause. Include: what they experienced, current status, next update time. Facts: [same as above]
Ops team update (internal, technical)
Write an internal update for our payments operations team. They need to know: which payment batches are affected, what manual steps (if any) they need to take right now, and what to tell customers who call in. Be specific and actionable.

Phase 3: Post-mortem

After the incident is resolved, use Claude to turn raw notes and Slack threads into a structured post-mortem document.

Post-mortem prompt
Write a post-mortem document for the following incident. Use this structure: Summary, Timeline, Root Cause, Impact, What Went Well, What Went Wrong, Corrective Actions (with owner and due date for each). Raw notes / Slack thread: [Paste your notes here] Additional context: - Total payments affected: [number] - Financial exposure: [amount] - Time to detect: [duration] - Time to resolve: [duration] - Was there a regulatory notification obligation? [yes/no/pending]
Corrective action refinement
Here are the corrective actions from our post-mortem. For each one, help me make it more specific and actionable — it should have a clear owner role (not a name), a concrete deliverable, and a realistic due date. Actions: [Paste your draft corrective actions]

Important: what NOT to paste into Claude

During an incident, do not paste real customer data, account numbers, routing numbers, or transaction IDs into Claude. Describe the problem in aggregate terms — number of payments affected, dollar ranges, failure codes — rather than sharing individual payment records. Check your company's AI usage policy before your first incident; knowing the rules in advance means you won't have to think about it when the pressure is on.
